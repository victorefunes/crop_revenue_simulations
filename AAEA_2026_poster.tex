\begin{block}{Motivation}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Operating credit is the financial lifeline of row-crop farms, yet credit-risk models treat revenue as one aggregate stream and ignore how rotation history shapes next year's yields.
  \item This study quantifies how three Illinois rotation plans differ in cash-flow delinquency risk (the borrower's problem) and peak credit demand (the lender's problem) over a six-year horizon.
\end{itemize}
\end{block}

\begin{block}{Research questions}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Does a more complex rotation improve financial outcomes, or do agronomic gains hit diminishing returns versus simple alternation?
  \item What is the 6-year probability of at least one operating-loan delinquency, conditioning yields on the full rotation history?
  \item How does the negative price--yield correlation change those probabilities?
\end{enumerate}
\end{block}

\begin{block}{Data \& methods}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Yields}: QDANN yield-mapping model, every IL field 2008--2022; productivity defined by quantiles of this yield distribution.
  \item \textbf{Rotations}: 6-year histories from the Cropland Data Layer (CDL).
  \item \textbf{Weather/soil}: GRIDMET, TerraClimate (VPD, PDSI), gSSURGO.
  \item \textbf{Costs}: 2024 Farmdoc Crop Budgets (vary by region and productivity in Central IL).
  \item \textbf{Prices}: grain-elevator prices; Gaussian copula embeds the negative price--yield correlation, calibrated at field level ($\rho^{*} \approx -0.354$).
  \item \textbf{Credit model}: spring origination $\rightarrow$ 9-month accrual $\rightarrow$ harvest repayment $\rightarrow$ balance rollover. $N = 50{,}000$ Monte Carlo paths per cell.
\end{itemize}
\end{block}

\begin{block}{Findings}
\begin{enumerate}

  \item \textbf{Rotation resets debt, not risk.} A soy harvest retires accumulated
  rollover balance because soy is planted on a lower spring loan. This resets
  year-end debt to near-zero before the next corn run --- periodically arresting
  the debt-compounding process rather than smoothing risk uniformly.

  % FIXED: original finding 2 ("compounds ... more cash on balance sheet") was a
  % robustness result elevated to a headline. Replaced with the paper's actual
  % second headline finding: price dominance and Shapley decomposition.
  \item \textbf{Price risk dominates; the hedge is modest.} A Shapley decomposition
  assigns 92--100\% of delinquency causation to price uncertainty across all nine
  region $\times$ zone cells. The natural hedge reduces delinquency by only 1--2
  percentage points --- its smallest benefit accruing to rotation borrowers, who
  have already reset carried debt in soy years.

  \item \textbf{Rotation and the natural hedge are partial substitutes.}
  Continuous corn relies on the hedge because it never retires its rollover
  balance; rotation plans already drive year-end debt to near-zero in soy years,
  so the hedge has little additional buffering to perform.

  \item \textbf{Rationing is rare, but rotation lowers peak demand.} Median peak
  loan demand clusters at \$500--550/ac, well below common credit ceilings. Rotation
  shifts the right tail down by roughly \$50/ac --- an observable, verifiable risk
  factor lenders can price into operating-credit underwriting.

\end{enumerate}
\end{block}
