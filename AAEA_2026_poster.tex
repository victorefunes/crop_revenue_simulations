\begin{block}{Motivation}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Operating credit is the financial lifeline of row-crop farms, yet credit-risk models treat revenue as one aggregate stream and ignore how rotation history shapes next year's yields.
    \item This study quantifies how three Illinois rotation plans differ in cash-flow delinquency risk (the borrower's problem) and peak credit demand (the lender's problem) over a six-year horizon.
\end{itemize}
\end{block}

\begin{block}{Research questions}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Does a more complex rotation improve financial outcomes, or do agronomic gains hit diminishing returns versus simple alternation?
    \item What is the 6-year probability of at least one operating-loan delinquency, conditioning yields on the full rotation history?
    \item How does the negative price–yield correlation change those probabilities?
\end{enumerate}
\end{block}

\begin{block}{Data \& methods}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Yields}: QDANN yield-mapping model, every IL field 2008–2022, productivity defined by quantiles of this yield distribution.
\item \textbf{Rotations}: 6-year histories from the Cropland Data Layer (CDL).
\item \textbf{Weather/soil}: GRIDMET, TerraClimate (VPD, PDSI), gSSURGO.
\item \textbf{Costs}: 2024 Farmdoc Crop Budgets (vary by region and productivity in Central IL).
\item \textbf{Prices \& costs}: grain-elevator prices; copula embeds the negative price–yield correlation.
\item \textbf{Credit model}: spring origination $\rightarrow$ 9-month accrual $\rightarrow$ harvest repayment $\rightarrow$ balance.

\end{itemize}
\end{block}

\begin{block}{Findings}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Rotation lowers risk not by smoothing it but by periodically arresting debt rollover: a soy harvest retires accumulated balance, resetting the farm to near-zero delinquency before the next corn run rebuilds it.
    \item The rotation advantage compounds: fewer delinquency events plus periodic soy harvests leave more cash on the balance sheet by year 6; even though corn has the higher gross revenue per acre.
    \item Rotation and the natural hedge are partial substitutes. Continuous corn relies on the hedge because it never retires its rollover balance; rotations already reset debt to near-zero in soy years, so the hedge has little left to do.
    \item Rationing is rare at these limits, but rotation lowers peak demand; an observable, verifiable risk factor lenders can price into operating-credit underwriting.
\end{enumerate}
\end{block}
